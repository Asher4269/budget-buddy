Personal Finance

Welcome to the learn more page of Budget Buddy!

Here we break down common personal finance concepts so you can make informed decisions and build a stronger financial future.

Learn More: Emergency Funds

What is an Emergency Fund?

An emergency fund is a pool of money set aside specifically for unexpected expenses—like medical bills, car repairs, or sudden job loss. It acts as a financial safety net so you don’t have to rely on credit cards or loans during stressful situations.

How much should one save?

A common recommendation is to save 3–6 months of essential living expenses. If your income is unstable or you have dependents, aiming closer to 6 months provides more security.

Where do I store the money?

Emergency funds should be kept in a safe, easily accessible account such as a high-yield savings account or money market account. The goal is liquidity, not high returns.

When do I use the money?

Use your emergency fund only for true emergencies—unexpected, necessary, and urgent expenses. Planned purchases or discretionary spending should not come from this fund.

Learn More: Investments

Stocks

Stocks represent partial ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you're investing in that company’s future performance. Stocks can offer high returns, but they also come with higher risk due to market fluctuations.

Index Funds

Index funds are collections of stocks designed to track a specific market index (like the S&P 500). They provide broad diversification, lower fees, and are often considered a strong long-term investment strategy.

Mutual Funds

Mutual funds pool money from many investors to purchase a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other assets. They are managed by professionals and typically charge management fees.

Learn More: Retirement

What is retirement?

Retirement refers to the stage of life when you stop working and rely on savings and investments for income. Retirement accounts are special investment accounts with tax advantages, designed to help your money grow over time until you reach retirement age.

Traditional vs. Roth

Traditional accounts provide tax benefits now—you contribute pre-tax income and pay taxes when you withdraw in retirement. Roth accounts do the opposite: you contribute after-tax income, but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.

Individual vs. 401k

Individual retirement accounts (IRAs) are opened independently, while 401(k) plans are employer-sponsored. 401(k)s often include employer matching and higher contribution limits, while IRAs offer more investment flexibility.

529

A 529 plan is a tax-advantaged savings account designed for education expenses. Funds grow tax-free and can be used for tuition, housing, books, and other qualified education costs.

Learn More: Taxes

What are taxes?

Taxes are mandatory payments made to government entities to fund public services like infrastructure, education, and healthcare. Different types of taxes apply to income, purchases, and property.

Income Tax

Income tax is charged on the money you earn. Federal and state income taxes are typically progressive (based on income brackets), while Social Security and Medicare taxes are usually flat percentages.

Sales Tax

Sales tax is added to the price of goods and services at the point of purchase. The rate varies by location and is typically not included in the listed price.

Estate Tax

Estate tax applies to the transfer of wealth after death, but only for very large estates above federal exemption limits. Most people will not be affected by federal estate taxes.

Tax Planning

Tax planning involves legally minimizing your tax liability through strategies like deductions, credits, and proper use of tax-advantaged accounts. It focuses on efficiency, not avoidance of responsibility.

Learn More: Debt

Mortgage

A mortgage is a loan used to purchase real estate, typically repaid over 15–30 years. The property itself serves as collateral, meaning the lender can take ownership if payments are not made.

Auto Loans

Auto loans are used to finance vehicles. Because cars depreciate quickly, these loans can sometimes leave borrowers owing more than the car is worth, making careful budgeting important.

Credit

Credit allows you to borrow money with the agreement to repay it later, usually with interest. Responsible use—such as paying balances in full and on time—helps build financial flexibility and a strong credit history.

Learn More: Credit Score

What is a credit score?

A credit score is a numerical representation of your creditworthiness, typically ranging from 300 to 850. It helps lenders assess how likely you are to repay borrowed money.

How am I scored?

Credit scores are based on factors like payment history, credit utilization, length of credit history, types of credit, and recent inquiries. Consistent on-time payments and low balances are key to maintaining a strong score.
